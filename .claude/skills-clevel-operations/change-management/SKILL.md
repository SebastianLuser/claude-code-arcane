# Change Management Playbook

Most changes fail at implementation, not design. The ADKAR model tells you why and how to fix it.

## Core Model: ADKAR Adapted for Startups

### A — Awareness
People understand WHY the change is happening. **Mistake:** Communicating the WHAT before the WHY. A 5-minute video from the decision-maker explaining the "why" in plain language beats a formal announcement every time.

### D — Desire
People want to make the change happen. **Mistake:** Assuming communication creates desire. Answer "What's in it for me?" for each stakeholder group. Address fears directly.

### K — Knowledge
People know HOW to operate in the new world. **Mistake:** Announcing the change and assuming people will figure it out. Provide documentation, training, and a person to ask when stuck.

### A — Ability
People have the time, tools, and support to actually do things differently. **Mistake:** "We've trained everyone" does not mean "everyone can now do it." Training is knowledge; ability is practice.

### R — Reinforcement
The change sticks. The new behavior becomes the default. **Mistake:** Declaring victory at go-live. Only reinforcement creates adoption.

## Key Questions for Change Management

- "Who are the most skeptical people about this change? Have we talked to them directly?"
- "Do people understand why we're doing this, or just what we're doing?"
- "Is the old way still available? If so, people will use it."
- "Are leaders modeling the new behavior themselves?"
- "How many changes are we running simultaneously right now?"

## Red Flags

- Change announced on Friday afternoon (people stew over the weekend)
- No published FAQ or way to ask questions safely
- Old system/process still running 6 weeks after "go-live"
- Leaders exempted from the change they're asking everyone else to make
- No measurement of adoption — assuming go-live = success

# /em:hard-call — Framework for Decisions With No Good Options

**Command:** `/em:hard-call <decision>`

For the decisions that keep you up at 3am. Firing a co-founder. Laying off 20% of the team. Killing a product customers love. These decisions don't have a right answer — they have a less wrong answer.

## Why These Decisions Are Hard

Not because the data is unclear. They're hard because: real people are affected, you've been avoiding the decision, it's irreversible, and your judgment is clouded by your feelings.

**Most hard decisions are late decisions.** The longer you avoid, the worse it gets.

## The Framework

### Step 1: The Reversibility Test
- **Reversible** — try it, learn, adjust
- **Partially reversible** — painful to undo but possible
- **Irreversible** — cannot be undone (higher bar for certainty)

**If you're treating a reversible decision like it's irreversible, you're avoiding it.**

### Step 2: The 10/10/10 Framework
- **10 minutes from now**: How will you feel?
- **10 months from now**: Will the problem be solved?
- **10 years from now**: Will this have been the right call?

### Step 3: The Andy Grove Test
"If we got replaced tomorrow and a new CEO came in, what would they do?" If the answer is clear to an outsider, the question is: why haven't you done it yet?

### Step 4: Stakeholder Impact Mapping
Map who's affected and how for each option. This isn't about finding the option that hurts nobody — there isn't one.

### Step 5: The Pre-Announcement Test
Write the announcement. If you can't write it, you're not ready to make the decision. Writing it forces you to confront the reality.

### Step 6: The Communication Plan
- **Who needs to know first** (the person directly affected, before anyone else)
- **How you'll tell them** (in person when possible, never via email for personal impact)
- **What comes next** (give them a clear picture)

## Decision-Specific Frameworks

**Firing a Co-Founder:** Is this performance or values? Have you been explicit about the problem? What do the cap table and legal implications look like? See `references/hard_things.md`.

**Layoffs:** Cut once, cut deep, cut with dignity. Two rounds destroys trust.

**Pivoting:** Pivots should be pulled by evidence of new opportunity, not pushed by failure.

**Killing a Product Line:** What happens to customers? What's the migration path? Is "sell it" or "spin it out" better than killing?

## The Avoiding-It Test

You're avoiding a hard call if: you've thought about it every week for a month, you're hoping it resolves itself, you've had the conversation in your head but not in real life.

**The cost of delay is almost always higher than the cost of the decision.**
